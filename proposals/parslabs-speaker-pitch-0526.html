Best practices for AI-assisted coding
You don't need to become a programmer. You need to know enough about how software works to direct AI effectively. Here's what that looks like in practice.
The foundation I teach non-coders
01
A browser speaks three languages
HTML = structure · CSS = style · JavaScript = behavior. When you ask for a login form, the AI outputs all three. If you don't know what each part does, you can't say "the button works but the colors are wrong" — you'll just accept whatever it gives you.
02
Data lives somewhere else
Most useful tools need to save, load, or share information. That data doesn't live in the webpage — it lives in a backend like Google Sheets, Airtable, or a database. Without this concept, you waste hours trying to make a page remember things it structurally can't.
03
Everything has a "state"
A button can be enabled or disabled. A task can be pending or done. A form can be empty or submitted. When you learn to say "preserve the state when I reload", the AI can give you something that works like an app, not a picture of one.
04
AI is a fast intern, not a mind reader
You give instructions. It produces code. If the code is wrong, you don't start over — you point to what's wrong and redirect. The skill is not writing perfect prompts. It's recognizing a mistake and saying "the button is at the top, move it to the bottom."
Most teams I work with go from "I don't know where to start" to shipping a working internal tool in one session — because once these four concepts click, the AI becomes an extension of their thinking, not a black box. 04 / 04
AI implementation risks
As both a practitioner and an investor, I see AI adoption from two angles. Most failures are not technical. They happen when systems are optimized for efficiency without accounting for how the team actually works or what they actually need to retain.
The problemA client came to me wanting full AI automation across their knowledge workflow. The initial build could do everything: intake, summarize, categorize, draft, and deliver. From a pure efficiency standpoint, it worked.
What we foundThe client had explicitly said their priority was learning retention, encoding knowledge themselves, not just having it processed for them. Full automation was actively working against their goal. The AI was doing so much that they were not absorbing anything.
The redesignWe rebuilt the system so AI functioned as backup and reference, not as primary processor. The client still interacts with material first. The system supports that process rather than replacing it. Same infrastructure, completely different design philosophy.
This is what I mean by building to the team's goals, not just their output. Efficiency is not always the right metric.
